Parallelism in the Book of Mormon

Definition

Parallelism is a literary technique where ideas are repeated or contrasted in similar grammatical structures, creating rhythm and reinforcing meaning.

Historical Context

A cornerstone of Hebrew poetry (e.g., Psalms), parallelism was used to aid memorization and emphasize key teachings, a tradition reflected in the Book of Mormon.

Example: 2 Nephi 9:28-29
O that cunning plan of the evil one!
O the vainness, and the frailties, and the foolishness of men!
When they are learned they think they are wise,
And they hearken not unto the counsel of God

Analysis

The repetition intensifies the warning against pride, while the contrast highlights the folly of human wisdom versus divine counsel.

Example: Lehi's Dream (1 Nephi 8, 11-15)
And I saw a tree, whose fruit was desirable to make one happy
And I beheld that the fruit thereof was white, to exceed all the whiteness that I had ever seen
And as I partook of the fruit, it filled my soul with exceedingly great joy
Wherefore, I began to be desirous that my family should partake of it also

Analysis

The parallelism emphasizes the transformative nature of spiritual experience, contrasting individual joy with the desire for collective understanding.

Example: The Good Shepherd (Alma 5)
And now behold, I ask of you, my brethren of the church
Have ye spiritually been born of God?
Have ye received his image in your countenances?
Have ye experienced this mighty change in your hearts?

Analysis

The parallel structure emphasizes the transformative power of spiritual rebirth, creating a rhythmic series of probing questions that challenge personal spiritual state.

Example: The Two Churches (1 Nephi 14)
And it came to pass that I saw two churches
One church was the church of the Lamb of God
And the other church was the church of the devil
Which church seeketh to destroy the saints of God

Analysis

The parallelism creates a stark contrast between two spiritual paths, emphasizing the fundamental division between good and evil through repetitive structure.

Example: The Liahona (Alma 37)
And now, my son, I would that ye should remember
That these things are not without a type and a shadow
For as our fathers were slothful to give heed
These miracles of power were stopped

Analysis

The parallelism underscores the importance of attentiveness and faith, contrasting remembrance with neglect and the consequences of spiritual complacency.

Example: The Light of Christ (Alma 26, Mosiah 16:9)
The light of Christ bringeth all men to a knowledge of God
And which lighteth every man that cometh into the world
And which is in all things, and is through all things
And is the law by which all things are governed

Analysis

The parallelism emphasizes the universal and pervasive nature of the Light of Christ, creating a rhythmic description of its omnipresent and governing power.

Example: The Vineyard & Laborers (Jacob 5, Alma 13)
And the Lord of the vineyard said unto his servant
Let us prune down this tree and dig about it
Peradventure it shall cast forth its young and tender branches
And we shall not perish when the season cometh

Analysis

The parallelism illustrates the careful, nurturing relationship between the divine and his creation, using agricultural metaphor to convey spiritual cultivation and hope.

Example: The Way (2 Nephi 31)
And now, I would ask of you, my beloved brethren
Whereon the path which leadeth to eternal life
And how is it known that ye are on the path?
Behold, it is by the witness of the Spirit

Analysis

The parallelism emphasizes the spiritual journey, contrasting external questioning with internal spiritual confirmation, creating a rhythmic exploration of faith.

Example: The Wicked Chains (2 Nephi 28, Alma 12)
And others will he pacify, and lull them away into carnal security
And they shall say: All is well in Zion
Yea, Zion prospereth, all is well
And thus the devil cheateth their souls

Analysis

The parallelism reveals the deceptive nature of spiritual complacency, contrasting false security with spiritual danger through rhythmic repetition.
